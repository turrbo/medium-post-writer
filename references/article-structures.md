## The Personal Essay

The most powerful format on Medium. Reader connects with your specific experience and finds universal truth in it.

### Flow
1. **The Moment** (1-2 paragraphs): Drop the reader into a specific scene. No setup, no context. Just the moment that this article exists because of
2. **The Context** (2-3 paragraphs): Now zoom out. Why were you in that moment? What led here?
3. **The Mess** (3-5 paragraphs): The complicated middle. Not a clean narrative -- the contradictions, the failed attempts, the embarrassing parts
4. **The Shift** (1-2 paragraphs): Something changed. Not a revelation -- more like a slow tilt. Maybe you're not even sure when it happened
5. **The Now** (1-2 paragraphs): Where you are today. Still figuring it out. Something resolved, something didn't

### Key Principles
- Start in the middle of the action, not the beginning of the story
- The "lesson" emerges from the story -- never announce it
- End with what you still don't know
- Include at least one detail you almost didn't share
- The specific IS the universal. The more particular your story, the more people relate

### Example Flow
"I sat in my car in the parking lot for 20 minutes before going into work." -> "I'd been at this company for 3 years." -> "The thing nobody tells you about burnout is..." -> "I started leaving at 5pm. It felt criminal." -> "I still feel guilty sometimes. I'm working on that."

## The How-To (Experience-Driven)

Not a tutorial. A how-to that's really a story about trying, failing, and eventually finding something that works (mostly).

### Flow
1. **The Problem (Yours)** (1-2 paragraphs): A specific instance of the problem. Not "many people struggle with..." but "I have 47 tabs open and I can't find the one I need"
2. **What Didn't Work** (2-3 paragraphs): The failed attempts. Be specific. Name the apps, the methods, the advice that didn't stick. This is where trust is built
3. **What Actually Worked** (3-5 paragraphs): The thing you do now. Describe it in enough detail to replicate, but frame it as YOUR experience, not a prescription
4. **The Caveats** (1-2 paragraphs): Why this might not work for everyone. What you're still struggling with. The limitations you've noticed
5. **Where You Are** (1 paragraph): Current state. Still using it? Modified it? Not perfect but better?

### Key Principles
- Your failures are more valuable than your solution
- "This might not work for you" is more trustworthy than "this will change your life"
- Include the embarrassingly simple detail ("I just... started writing things down")
- Show the iteration: version 1 failed, version 2 was better, version 3 is what I do now
- The how-to is the story. The story is the how-to. Don't separate them

### Example Flow
"I had 3 different to-do apps and none of them had my actual to-do list." -> "Tried GTD. Lasted 4 days. Tried Pomodoro. Lasted 2." -> "The dumb thing that works: a sticky note with 3 things." -> "It doesn't work on busy days. Or Mondays." -> "I've been doing this for 6 months and I still forget the sticky note sometimes."

## The Opinion Piece

Strong take, grounded in experience. Not an argument -- a perspective.

### Flow
1. **The Observation** (1-2 paragraphs): Something you've noticed. Specific, not abstract. A pattern, a contradiction, a thing that doesn't add up
2. **Why It Bothers You** (1-2 paragraphs): The emotional engine. Why do you care about this? Personal stake
3. **The Evidence** (3-4 paragraphs): What you've seen, experienced, or found that supports your take. Not exhaustive proof -- selected moments that illustrate the point
4. **The Complication** (1-2 paragraphs): The counterargument you take seriously. The part of your take that might be wrong. Show you've thought about this
5. **Your Position** (1-2 paragraphs): Where you land, with acknowledged uncertainty. "I still think X, but I understand why people disagree"

### Key Principles
- Lead with observation, not conclusion
- Your strongest argument is your experience, not your logic
- Include the strongest counterargument and engage with it honestly
- End with conviction + humility, not certainty
- The reader should feel like you've thought hard about this, not that you're performing outrage

## The Technical Article

Deep dive on a specific technical topic, made human through personal experience.

### Flow
1. **The Problem I Had** (1-2 paragraphs): A specific technical problem in a real project. Not a hypothetical
2. **The Wrong Approach First** (2-3 paragraphs): What you tried that didn't work. Why you thought it would work. What went wrong (with code/details)
3. **The Realization** (1-2 paragraphs): What you figured out. The conceptual shift, not just the code fix
4. **The Solution** (3-5 paragraphs): Technical detail here. Code, architecture, diagrams. But embedded in the story of implementing it
5. **What I'd Do Differently** (1-2 paragraphs): Hindsight. Limitations of your approach. What you're still not happy with

### Key Principles
- The human story wraps the technical content
- "I was wrong about X" is more useful than "X is the right way"
- Include the actual error messages, the debugging dead ends
- Code should be real, tested, and honest about edge cases
- End with what's still imperfect -- readers trust you more

## The Reflection

Looking back at something with the distance of time. Not advice -- processing.

### Flow
1. **The Trigger** (1-2 paragraphs): What made you think about this now? A conversation, an anniversary, stumbling on old photos/code/emails
2. **Then** (2-3 paragraphs): What it was like at the time. Specific details. How it felt, not how you now understand it
3. **The Gap** (1-2 paragraphs): What changed between then and now. Time, perspective, new information
4. **Now** (2-3 paragraphs): How you see it differently. What you understand that you didn't. What you still don't
5. **The Unresolved** (1 paragraph): What remains. Not everything gets processed. Not everything makes sense

### Key Principles
- The distance between "then" and "now" IS the story
- Don't moralize about your past self
- Include what you miss about the old way, not just what's better now
- "I still don't understand why" is a valid conclusion
- Time markers anchor the reflection: "It was 2019. I had just..."

## The Story

Pure narrative. Something happened and you're telling it. The meaning, if any, is implied.

### Flow
1. **Entry Point** (1 paragraph): Drop into the scene. Sensory detail. Immediate
2. **Unfolding** (variable): Let the events happen. Don't summarize -- show
3. **The Turn** (1-2 paragraphs): Something shifts. Could be dramatic, could be tiny
4. **After** (1-2 paragraphs): What happened next. Or what didn't happen
5. **Exit** (1 paragraph): End close to the action. Don't pull back to reflect. Let the reader do that

### Key Principles
- Show, show, show. Don't tell the reader what to feel
- Dialogue, if included, should sound exactly like people talk
- Physical details ground the story (the cold coffee, the fluorescent light, the specific chair)
- Not everything needs to be explained
- The best stories end slightly too soon

## Anti-Patterns (Structures That Feel AI-Generated)

### The Five-Paragraph Essay
Introduction -> Point 1 -> Point 2 -> Point 3 -> Conclusion. Learned in school, forgotten by real writers. Every AI uses this structure because it was in the training data.
**Fix:** Let section lengths vary wildly. Some ideas need 1 paragraph. Some need 5.

### The Perfect Arc
Clear problem -> clear journey -> clear resolution. Real life doesn't arc this cleanly.
**Fix:** Leave something unresolved. Include a tangent. End before the resolution.

### The Listicle Disguised as an Essay
"5 things I learned" but with paragraphs between the numbers. It's still a listicle.
**Fix:** If the insights are connected, tell the story that contains them. If they're not, maybe it should just be a list (and that's fine).

### The Thesis-Evidence-Conclusion
Academic paper structure. Every paragraph supports the main argument. Too orderly.
**Fix:** Include a paragraph that complicates your argument. Contradict yourself once.

### The Mirror Ending
Opening scene, repeated at the end with new meaning. Creative writing 101. AI loves it.
**Fix:** End somewhere new. Or reference the opening obliquely, not symmetrically.

## Pacing and Rhythm

### Paragraph Length Variation
- Use one-sentence paragraphs for emphasis. They create white space and impact
- Follow a long, dense paragraph with a short one. The contrast creates rhythm
- Never write 4 paragraphs of the same length in a row

### Sentence Length Variation
- Short sentences create urgency. They hit hard.
- Long sentences, the kind that meander through a thought with multiple clauses and pauses and occasionally forget where they were going, mimic how people actually think
- Alternate. That's the whole trick

### Section Length Variation
- Not every section needs to be the same size
- A 1-paragraph section between two 5-paragraph sections creates surprise
- The shortest section often contains the most important insight

### The Breathing Room
- White space between sections lets readers absorb
- Not every paragraph needs to advance the argument
- A seemingly irrelevant aside can be the most memorable part of the article
- Silence (ending a thought without explaining it) is powerful

### Reading Time Targets
| Type | Sweet Spot | Maximum | Notes |
|------|-----------|---------|-------|
| Personal essay | 5-7 min | 10 min | Shorter is usually stronger |
| How-to | 5-8 min | 10 min | Depends on complexity |
| Opinion piece | 4-6 min | 8 min | Say it and get out |
| Technical article | 6-10 min | 15 min | Depth is expected |
| Reflection | 4-6 min | 8 min | Brevity serves reflection |
| Story | 5-8 min | 12 min | Depends on the story |
