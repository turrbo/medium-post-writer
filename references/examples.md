# Good vs Bad Examples (Medium Edition)

## Article Openings

### BAD (AI-Written):
"In today's rapidly evolving tech landscape, burnout has become an increasingly prevalent issue among software developers. As someone who has navigated this challenging terrain, I want to share my experience and the valuable lessons I've learned along the way."

### GOOD (Human-Written):
"I stopped being able to type on a Thursday. Not physically -- my hands worked fine. I just sat at my desk, cursor blinking, and couldn't make myself start. I'd been a developer for 11 years and I couldn't write a for loop. I went to the bathroom and sat on the floor for a while."

**Why the second works:**
- Specific day (Thursday)
- Physical detail (cursor blinking, sat on the floor)
- Unexpected vulnerability (sat on the bathroom floor)
- No framing or setup -- just drops you into the moment
- "for a while" is vaguely ominous
- No "I want to share" or "lessons learned"

---

### BAD Opening (How-To Article):
"If you've ever struggled with productivity, you're not alone. Millions of professionals face the same challenge daily. In this comprehensive guide, I'll walk you through a straightforward framework that has genuinely transformed my workflow."

### GOOD Opening (How-To Article):
"I've tried every productivity system. GTD, Pomodoro, bullet journals, three different apps, a whiteboard in my office that I stopped updating after two weeks. None of them stuck. What finally worked was so embarrassing I almost didn't write this."

**Why the second works:**
- Lists specific failures (not just "I struggled")
- Self-deprecating detail (whiteboard abandoned after 2 weeks)
- Hooks with "embarrassing" -- you want to know what it is
- Doesn't announce the guide -- just tells the story
- "Almost didn't write this" signals authenticity

---

## Article Middle Sections

### BAD (AI-Written):
"The key insight I discovered was that consistency matters more than intensity. By implementing a sustainable routine and leveraging the right tools, I was able to significantly improve my output. Furthermore, the systematic approach allowed me to identify and eliminate inefficiencies that had been holding me back."

### GOOD (Human-Written):
"The thing that actually helped -- and I know this sounds dumb -- was writing down what I did every day. Not goals. Not tasks. Just what I actually did between 9 and 5.

Turns out I spent about 3 hours a day on things I thought took 30 minutes. Email alone was killing me. Not writing emails. Reading emails I didn't need to read and then thinking about them for 20 minutes.

I don't have a system for fixing this. I just stopped opening email before noon. It was deeply uncomfortable for about a week and then nobody noticed."

**Why the second works:**
- "I know this sounds dumb" -- self-aware
- Distinguishes what it's NOT (goals, tasks) before what it IS
- Specific number (3 hours vs. perceived 30 minutes)
- The real insight sneaks in (reading emails, then THINKING about them)
- No "system" -- just a blunt behavioral change
- "Nobody noticed" undercuts the drama perfectly

## Article Conclusions

### BAD (AI-Written):
"Looking back on this experience, I've come to realize that burnout isn't just about working too hard -- it's about losing sight of why you started in the first place. If there's one takeaway from my journey, it's this: prioritize your well-being, set clear boundaries, and remember that your career is a marathon, not a sprint. I hope sharing my story helps others who might be navigating similar challenges."

### GOOD (Human-Written):
"I went back to work eventually. Different company, same kind of job. People ask if I 'solved' burnout and I don't know how to answer that. I take Fridays off now. I close my laptop at 5 most days. I still sit at my desk sometimes and feel the blank thing happening and I get up and walk around the block.

I don't know if that's recovery or just management. Maybe there isn't a difference."

**Why the second works:**
- No lesson. No takeaway. Just what happened
- Specific behavioral changes (Fridays off, laptop closed at 5)
- Admits the problem isn't solved ("the blank thing" still happens)
- The last line is genuinely uncertain
- "Maybe there isn't a difference" invites the reader to think, not agree
- Ends with a question the writer doesn't know the answer to

## Tool Mentions

### BAD (Too Promotional):
"That's when I discovered ToolName. This powerful platform genuinely streamlined my entire workflow. The comprehensive features allowed me to automate repetitive tasks and focus on what truly matters. I highly recommend giving it a try."

### GOOD (Properly Subtle):
"My friend set up some kind of automation that pulls my tasks into a spreadsheet every morning. I think it uses ToolName? I honestly don't know how it works. I just know that on days when it breaks -- maybe once a month -- I feel the old chaos creeping back in, and that's how I know it's doing something."

**Why the second works:**
- Attributed to someone else ("my friend set it up")
- Uncertainty about the tool ("I think it uses?")
- Doesn't understand it ("don't know how it works")
- Acknowledges it breaks
- The proof is emotional (feeling chaos), not metric-based

## Title and Subtitle Pairs

### BAD Titles:
- "My Comprehensive Guide to Overcoming Burnout: Lessons From a 10-Year Tech Veteran"
- "How I Revolutionized My Productivity (And You Can Too)"
- "The Truth About Remote Work That Nobody Is Talking About"
- "5 Essential Strategies for Navigating Career Transitions"

### GOOD Titles:
- "I Couldn't Type on a Thursday"
- "Every Productivity System Failed Me Except the Embarrassing One"
- "I Quit My Job. It Didn't Fix Anything"
- "What I Actually Do Between 9 and 5"

### BAD Subtitles:
- "A comprehensive exploration of modern burnout and strategies for recovery"
- "Insights from my journey through career transformation"

### GOOD Subtitles:
- "On sitting on the bathroom floor at work and what happened after"
- "Or: how I discovered I spend 3 hours a day doing nothing useful"
- "And what happened when I tried to build one from scratch anyway"

---

## Subheading Styles

### BAD Subheadings (Too Formal):
- "The Fundamental Challenge of Modern Productivity"
- "Implementing a Sustainable Solution"
- "Key Takeaways and Next Steps"

### GOOD Subheadings (Human):
- "The part where everything falls apart"
- "What I actually did (it's embarrassing)"
- "I still don't have this figured out"
- "Anyway"
- "The spreadsheet era"
- "Things got worse before they got better. Then they got worse again"

---

## Emotional Authenticity Markers

1. **Contradictions:** "I love being a developer" + "I fantasize about never touching a computer again"
2. **Specificity over generality:** "I applied to 127 jobs" not "I applied to many jobs"
3. **Sensory details:** "The cursor blinking. The hum of the fluorescent light"
4. **Admitting not knowing:** "I have no idea if this actually works or if I'm just in a better headspace"
5. **The almost-didn't-include detail:** "I cried in my car after the meeting. I'm 38"
6. **Ongoing uncertainty:** The problem isn't resolved. The article ends in the middle of the story
7. **Self-interruption:** "But actually -- that's not what this is about. Or maybe it is"
8. **Time markers:** "This was a Tuesday" / "It was maybe 3pm" / "Two weeks later"
